Importance of International Mother Language Day

21 February is observed as International Mother Language Day, a day dedicated to promoting the preservation and protection of all languages. The date commemorates the martyrs of the 1952 Bengali Language Movement, who lost their lives defending their mother tongue. The movement arose in protest against the government's decision to declare Urdu as the only official language, disregarding the Bengali-speaking majority of East Pakistan (present-day Bangladesh). Today, nearly 40% of the world's population lacks access to education in a language they speak or understand. International Mother Language Day serves as an important reminder to celebrate linguistic diversity and promote inclusive, multilingual education worldwide.

In Bangladesh, 21 February 1952 is the anniversary of the day when the Bengalis of the then-Pakistani province of East Bengal (now Bangladesh) fought for recognition of their Bengali language as an official state language instead of a provincial language. The day is also observed by Indian Bengalis in the states of West Bengal, Assam, Jharkhand, and Tripura, as well as in the union territory of the Andaman and Nicobar Islands.

Language is the primary tool through which humans communicate, express their thoughts, and share experiences. It is not just a means of exchanging information, but also a cornerstone of personal and cultural identity. Through language, individuals connect with family, friends, and communities, fostering social bonds and creating a sense of belonging. Recognizing this, UNESCO emphasizes that the preservation of cultural and linguistic diversity is vital for building inclusive and sustainable societies worldwide. Yet, in today's rapidly globalizing world, countless languages are vanishing at an alarming rate. Every year, communities lose unique languages that carry generations of history, traditions, and wisdom.

The disappearance of a language is more than just the loss of words—it represents the erosion of cultural heritage, local knowledge, and a unique worldview. Without the ability to communicate in their native languages, many communities face difficulties in education, social participation, and even in understanding developments beyond their local environment. Protecting linguistic diversity, therefore, is not merely about saving words; it is about safeguarding the identity, knowledge, and resilience of entire cultures for future generations.

What We Should Remember

International Mother Language Day is not only a day of remembrance but also a call to action. On this day, we should:

  • Respect and cherish our mother language as a core part of our identity.
  • Honor the sacrifices of the language martyrs of 1952 who gave their lives for linguistic rights.
  • Promote linguistic diversity and support inclusive, multilingual education.
  • Recognize that every language carries unique culture, history, and wisdom.
  • Encourage younger generations to learn, speak, and preserve their native languages.
  • Stand against the extinction of languages and advocate for global cultural preservation.

Remembering these values helps us build a world that respects diversity, fosters understanding, and honors the legacy of those who fought for their language and identity.

How to Celebrate International Mother Language Day in Bangladesh

International Mother Language Day is celebrated across Bangladesh with respect, reflection, and cultural events. Here are some common ways people honor the day:

  • Visit Shaheed Minar: People visit the Shaheed Minar monument in Dhaka and other cities, often barefoot, to pay tribute to the language martyrs by placing flowers and wreaths.
  • Educational Programs: Schools and universities organize debates, essay competitions, and cultural programs to raise awareness about the importance of mother languages.
  • Cultural Events: Recitations of poems, songs, and plays related to the Language Movement are performed. Traditional music and performances showcase Bengali culture.
  • Flag Hoisting: National flags are hoisted at half-mast at government offices and educational institutions to honor the martyrs.
  • Moment of Silence: A moment of silence is observed at 8:00 AM in memory of the 1952 martyrs.
  • Media Broadcasts: TV and radio channels broadcast special programs, documentaries, and discussions about the Language Movement and linguistic diversity.
  • Community Activities: Local communities hold seminars, exhibitions, and storytelling sessions to educate the public about preserving the Bengali language and heritage.

By participating in these activities, people in Bangladesh celebrate the rich cultural heritage of their language, honor the bravery of the martyrs, and encourage younger generations to value and preserve their mother tongue.
